# Decision Making Under Pressure and Uncertainty

Being pragmatic in the right context and highly diligent in another is one of the most critical skills to move fast and be ready to scale.

---

## Why It Matters

| Dimension | Insight |
|-----------|---------|
| **Speed** | 37% of an employee's time is spent on decision making — 18.5% of total working time is wasted due to ineffective decisions. |
| **Quality** | Not every decision will be perfect, but following certain practices leads to [38x better decisions](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/decision-making-in-the-age-of-urgency), measured in growth and return. |

---

## The Challenge

| Risk | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Too many shortcuts** | Within weeks or months, issues compound until you need to redo everything. |
| **Too diligent** | Not moving fast enough — which can be fatal for a startup. |

## Principles

| # | Principle | Description |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | **Start with the opinionated end in mind** | Think about the end-user's goal and work backwards to have a clear direction. |
| 2 | **Seek dissenting opinions** | Good decisions are built on disagreement, not consensus. Actively seek out intelligent people who think differently — especially those closest to the work. Dissent converts a plausible answer into the right one. *(Drucker, Dalio; [McKinsey: 2–3x more likely to be a winning org on big-bet decisions])* Note: probing and challenge is not second-guessing — it improves quality over time and does not transfer decision authority. Keep debate constructive; unlimited nitpicking undermines the process. |
| 3 | **Boundary conditions** | Define the minimum conditions the decision must satisfy before evaluating options — do not over-analyse. Ask: what fully satisfies the specifications *before* compromises and concessions are made? Understand the game you are in and where the value lies: build a clear framework for what you evaluate, why you evaluate it, and how you weight it. Satisfy, don't maximise. |
| 4 | **Reuse vs. invent** | If there are proven best practices and it is not a competitive edge, do not waste time inventing something new — reuse what already exists (but understand why it's a best practice). |
| 4 | **Simple vs. Complex** | Ask yourself: what is the simplest way to achieve your goal? But you must still achieve your goal — being too pragmatic can mean you fail to deliver. |
| 5 | **Not universal** | Pragmatism is not universally good. Each context decides the level of pragmatism you should apply. |
| 6 | **Is this the road that gets us there?** | Imagine what your company looks like at maximum success, then ask: "Is this the road that will get us there?" *(Matt Miller, Sequoia)* |
| 7 | **Outsource decisions through context** | Empower people with enough context to decide on your behalf. Get them involved in how you think about strategy, give them space to ask questions and bring ideas. |
| 8 | **Set a clear decision threshold** | When delegating, make sure people understand the impact of the decision and at what point they must consult you before making a call. |
| 9 | **Don't always penalise threshold overruns** | If people overstep the decision threshold and the decision was good — extend the threshold. If bad — train and maintain it. Never lower the threshold; that slows decision making. Reconsider only if the impact was severely negative. |
| 10 | **Reversibility / Impact Matrix** | Low impact + high reversibility → decide fast. High impact + low reversibility → slow down and consult. |
| 11 | **What would you recommend your worst enemy?** | A useful lens to cut through bias and surface what you actually believe is the right call. |
| 12 | **Decide against everything not absolutely necessary** | Stay focused by cutting what is not essential. *(Scott Belsky — doing half of everything)* Note: small team favors are not nice-to-have — a great team dynamic gets you 10x further. |
| 13 | **Intuition works where experience is deep** | Trust intuition in areas you know well. Where you lack experience, don't trust it — except for future vision, where gut feel often outperforms expert opinion. |
| 14 | **On future vision, trust your gut over experts** | Future visions are inherently uncertain. Expert opinion is often anchored to the present — your gut on direction is more reliable. |

## Devil of your mind (biases)

| Bias | Description | Antidote |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking information that confirms what you already believe, ignoring contradicting evidence. | Start with an opinion to direct your search, but actively seek out opposing data and dissenting voices. |
| **Groupthink** | The pressure to conform to group consensus kills dissent and produces mediocre decisions. | Assign a devil's advocate. Separate idea generation from evaluation. Reward people who disagree. |
| **Authority bias** | Over-weighting the opinion of senior people, even when those closest to the work know better. | Make decisions as close to the action as possible. Explicitly ask for input from those doing the hands-on work. |
| **Anchoring** | Over-relying on the first piece of information encountered, making all subsequent judgement relative to it. | Generate your own estimate before hearing others'. Expose multiple reference points before committing. |
| **Sunk cost fallacy** | Continuing down a path because of past investment rather than future value. | Evaluate decisions forward-only: "If we hadn't already invested, would we start this today?" |
| **Overconfidence bias** | Overestimating the accuracy of your own judgement, especially in unfamiliar domains. | Trust intuition only where experience is deep. In new domains, seek external calibration. |
| **Availability bias** | Overweighting recent, vivid, or memorable events when assessing probability or risk. | Slow down on emotionally salient decisions. Ask: "Am I reacting to what just happened, or to the base rate?" |
| **Status quo bias** | Preferring the current state simply because changing it feels risky — even when change is clearly better. | Reframe the decision: "If we were starting from scratch today, would we choose this?" |

## Angel of your mind (emotions)

Emotions calibrate decision making for complexity you cannot comprehend with logic — especially the future impact of decisions that affect your social life. Your body reacts with feelings to a decision, positive or negative, as a signal worth reading.

### Trust your gut — but verify it

There is a continuum of how much to rely on intuition. Emotions are key inputs, even when experienced only subconsciously during the decision-making process.

| Lens | Insight |
|------|---------|
| **Cross-indexing** | Truly inspired decisions require seeing similar patterns across disparate fields. The ability to cross-index is what elevates intuitive skill from good to sublime. |
| **Gary Klein** | Take your gut feeling as an important data point, but then consciously and deliberately evaluate it — does it make sense in this context? Use strategies that help rule things out. That is the opposite of gathering information to confirm what your gut already said. |
| **Kahneman** | Under time pressure, follow intuition. But do not take intuitions at face value. Overconfidence is driven by the coherence of the story you can construct, not its validity. A simple, compelling narrative will feel confident regardless of how well grounded it is in reality. |
| **Pattern match** | If the situation follows a pattern you have experienced before, follow your intuition. Where the pattern is new, slow down and deliberate. |
