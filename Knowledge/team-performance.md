A framework for understanding yourself as a leader, reading your team, and creating the conditions for high performance.

## Team Archetypes

Understanding what motivates a person — what drives them to distinguish themselves and do good work.

| Archetype | Core Motivation | What They Need | What Breaks Them |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Profilieren** *(distinguish themselves)* | Recognition, standing out, building a reputation | Visible wins, credit for their work, opportunities to shine | Being invisible, work attributed to others |
| **Decent Job** | Stability, fair compensation, room to do good work | Time to excel, decent money, clear expectations | Constant pressure, unclear standards, underpayment |
| **Appreciation** | Feeling valued and respected | Regular acknowledgment, genuine feedback | Being ignored, taken for granted, criticized publicly |
| **Better** *(improve society)* | Purpose, positive impact beyond the company | Meaningful work, connection to a bigger mission | Purely commercial framing, lack of purpose narrative |
| **Leader** *(create impact)* | Influence, ownership, building something significant | Decision-making power, autonomy, scope to grow | Micromanagement, being sidelined, no growth path |

## Drivers: 6 Levels of Leadership

Leadership is not a single mode — it operates at different levels depending on what the team or situation needs. Effective leaders move deliberately between levels rather than defaulting to one.

### 1. Context & Strategy

Give the team the full picture so they can make good decisions without you.

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Pitch from the customer perspective (use visuals if possible) |
| 2 | Give the current decision — open room to challenge and ask questions |
| 3 | Explain why this decision — the thought process behind it |
| 4 | Financial impact if possible, otherwise business impact |
| 5 | How the future will look if this works |
| 6 | Details of the decision (flow charts, visuals, formulas, tech specs) |

### 2. Empower & Ownership

- **Don't be a role model by default.** As soon as you tell someone what to do, the employee loses ownership and you take over. If you want them to imitate you, be a role model. If you want them to develop their own strengths, let them find their own path. Every piece you invest in being perceived as a role model is also an investment in your own prison.
- **Don't do anything the employee can do on their own.** As soon as you help, the employee exits ownership and you take over.
  - Don't tell them to prepare more — let them feel the consequences.
  - Encourage them that they can do it.
  - Ensure the employee takes the decision; you are not willing to step in, only as support to a certain degree.
  - Ask: *What alternatives have you thought of? What happens if you do nothing? What can you do?*
  - If someone asks to go to a workshop, ask: *What do you expect from it?* Leave ownership on their side.
- **Create an environment of trust.** Open discussions, support, and respect.
- **Reward and penalty destroy ownership.** They take away the feeling of accountability.
- **On evaluation:** It is subjective — acknowledge that. The employee may have a different perspective. Appreciate other opinions because you only have your subjective view. The evaluation tells a lot about your own values. *"Was Sie von mir denken, geht mich nichts an." — Terry Cole-Whittaker*
- **Commitments:**
  - Only make them if you can fulfil them.
  - Only make them if they are important to you.
  - Write them down. Never break a commitment.
  - Commitments are better than orders — they go beyond orders. Both parties agree and they are only valuable as long as both see value in them.
  - If people make mistakes, don't penalize them — support them to be better.
- **Give decision power.** Make it explicit what decisions someone can make on their own.

### 3. Coach

- Let them reflect — ask questions, don't give answers.
- Give impulses to be better, not instructions on what to do.

### 4. Push

- Bring people to a healthy edge — cold water, but not drowning.
- Create urgency: why not today instead of tomorrow? Design urgency into every interaction.
- Good performers crave a culture of energy.

### 5. Team Appreciation & Fun

- Team first.
- Give credit to people publicly.
- Receive credit gracefully for good work.
- Make it enjoyable.

### 6. Strategy & Structure

- Define the mode of collaboration (e.g. Kanban, retros).
- Roadmapping and task management system.
- Repeat the mission pitch over and over.
- Focus and alignment on one priority — if you have several priorities, you have none.

## Potential Conflicts and Solutions

| # | Conflict | Solution |
|---|----------|----------|
| 1 | **No clear responsibility** | 1 person responsible — not more. |
| 2 | **Not clear what decisions can be made** | Ask: Is the decision reversible? What level of decision can be made without sparring? Consider financial impact, customer interaction, etc. Still do sparring to reach better decisions — but make the threshold explicit. |
| 3 | **Conflict between organizational and individual goals** | Recognize it. Create awareness that it is a balance — we try to accompany individual needs while ensuring company success. Ask each person what is important to them. |
| 4 | **Boundary of what to discuss** | Define what is discussed openly and what is not. Not talking can be dangerous, but talking can also overstep personal boundaries. Set the boundary explicitly. |

## Pressure Up or Down

### Step 1: Analyse the current stress level of each team member before acting.

---

### Heat Up (increase pressure)

1. **Weekly progress update meeting** — pair on the problem and discuss it; increase intensity over time.
2. **Every 6 weeks: senior leadership alignment** — team shows what they've done.
3. **Increase feedback frequency, but decrease intensity.**
   > *"It's Wednesday and I'm only seeing three opportunities in the funnel for this week. What time of day works best for you to input your meetings?"*
4. **Connect their behavior to something bigger.**
   > *"I had to go into the Regional Leadership Team meeting today with a pipeline that shows only about 30% of what it should show. The VP was alarmed. I had to assure him the data aren't representative — but I won't get away with that again."*
5. **Set ambitious milestones** — reachable at ~70% with good effort, close to 100% in god mode.
6. **Allow natural negative consequences for lack of action.** If you pick up the slack from poor performance to avoid a bad outcome, you reinforce that they don't need to change.
   > *"If you ask the Regional VP not to discuss any opportunities that are not in the system, your hold-outs will be left out of the discussion. Salespeople are fueled by posting wins — this loss of recognition might spur action."*
7. **Feedback tonality:** Be clear about what is not acceptable and what is not an option.

---

### Heat Down (reduce pressure)

1. **Provide frequent positive feedback** — celebrate the small wins.
2. **Break the problem into smaller pieces.**
   > *"You're not climbing Everest; you're just getting to basecamp. This week, we're going to focus on the automotive sector. Let's get all of our automotive leads into the system."*
3. **Add structure to the problem.** Don't solve it for them — that sends the wrong messages and creates accountability issues. Go slightly further than normal in helping them think about how to tackle it.
4. **Model confidence.** Show with your words and body language that you believe everything will work out.
